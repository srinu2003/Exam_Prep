UNIT I

Research Problem Formulation & Data Analysis

1.1 Meaning of Research Problem

Research in its scientific sense is a systematic, controlled, empirical, and critical investigation of hypothetical propositions about presumed relations among natural or computational phenomena. According to Redman and Mory, research is a "systematized effort to gain new knowledge."

Definition: Meaning of Research Problem: A Research Problem refers to an explicit statement regarding a gap in existing knowledge, an operational bottleneck in technology, or a theoretical contradiction that requires systematic investigation, empirical data collection, and analytical reasoning to resolve.

1.2 Sources of Research Problem

Identifying a viable research problem is the fundamental first step in master's research. Primary sources of research problems include:

  • Literature Review & Published Gaps: Analyzing discussion sections and "Future Work" recommendations in top-tier peer-reviewed journals (IEEE Transactions, ACM Digital Library).
  • Technological Shifts & Paradigms: Emerging computing trends such as Quantum Machine Learning, Edge AI, and Zero-Trust Security architectures.
  • Industrial & Practical Bottlenecks: Practical challenges faced by industry (e.g. latency in autonomous perception or battery limits in IoT microcontrollers).
  • Interdisciplinary Intersections: Applying computer science models to biological data (Bioinformatics), financial systems (FinTech), or medical diagnostics.

1.3 Criteria & Characteristics of a Good Research Problem

A sound research problem must satisfy the S.M.A.R.T. Framework and specific academic criteria:

Criterion Description Computer Science / Engineering Context
Novelty & Originality Must contribute new insights, higher efficiency, or novel frameworks. Developing a transformer pruning algorithm reducing parameter size by 40% without accuracy loss.
Feasibility Solvable within time, budget, computation, and hardware bounds. Verifying GPU cluster availability (NVIDIA A100/H100) before attempting to train large language models.
Relevance & Impact Addresses a problem of clear economic, technical, or societal value. Improving real-time fraud detection accuracy in high-throughput payment transaction pipelines.
Testability & Measurability Variables must be quantifiable using standard benchmark metrics. Latency (ms), Throughput (req/sec), F1-Score, Memory Footprint (MB).
Ethical Acceptability Complies with data privacy regulations (GDPR) and safety laws. Ensuring medical imaging datasets are fully anonymized prior to model training.

1.4 Errors in Selecting a Research Problem

Critical Errors to Avoid in Problem Selection:
  • Over-broad Scope: Selecting a topic so vast (e.g. "Improving Cybersecurity using AI") that it cannot be investigated within time constraints.
  • Trivial / Over-researched Topics: Re-implementing established algorithms without introducing novel variations.
  • Ignoring Resource & Compute Limits: Formulating problems requiring unavailable hardware, proprietary datasets, or specialized labs.
  • Unscientific / Subjective Bias: Designing a problem to prove a pre-conceived subjective preference rather than objective empirical truth.

1.5 Scope and Objectives of Research Problem

Defining the Scope establishes explicit boundaries (delimitations) of what the research will and will not cover. Objectives must be split into primary and secondary goals supported by statistical hypotheses:

  • Null Hypothesis ($H_0$): Assumes no statistical significance or difference exists between the proposed method and the baseline ($H_0: \mu_{\text{proposed}} = \mu_{\text{baseline}}$).
  • Alternative Hypothesis ($H_1$): States that the proposed intervention yields statistically significant improvement ($H_1: \mu_{\text{proposed}} > \mu_{\text{baseline}}$).

1.6 Approaches of Investigation of Solutions for Research Problem

Research approaches dictate how data is gathered and logical conclusions drawn:

  • Deductive Approach: Top-down reasoning moving from general theory $\rightarrow$ hypothesis $\rightarrow$ empirical test $\rightarrow$ confirmation.
  • Inductive Approach: Bottom-up reasoning moving from specific observations $\rightarrow$ pattern discovery $\rightarrow$ tentative hypothesis $\rightarrow$ theory building.
  • Exploratory & Diagnostic Approaches: Investigating under-researched topics or diagnosing root causes of system failures.
  • Experimental & Quantitative Modeling: Controlled benchmarking, mathematical simulations, and algorithmic modeling.

graph TD A[Approaches of Investigation] --> B[Deductive Reasoning] A --> C[Inductive Reasoning] B --> B1[Theory / General Rule] B1 --> B2[Hypothesis Formulation] B2 --> B3[Empirical Testing] B3 --> B4[Confirmation / Rejection] C --> C1[Specific Observations] C1 --> C2[Pattern Discovery] C2 --> C3[Tentative Hypothesis] C3 --> C4[General Theory Building]

1.7 Data Collection, Analysis, and Interpretation

Primary Data Collection gathers original empirical data via experiments, sensor logs, user telemetry, or network packet traces. Secondary Data Collection extracts pre-existing data from repositories (Kaggle, UCI, IEEE DataPort).

KaTeX Data Analysis & Sample Size Formulas:

Cochran's Sample Size Formula for large populations ($N > 10,000$):

$$n_0 = \frac{Z^2 \cdot p \cdot (1 - p)}{e^2}$$

Yamane's Formula for finite population sample size calculation:

$$n = \frac{N}{1 + N(e)^2}$$

Student's t-test statistic for comparing algorithm performance means:

$$t = \frac{\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2}{\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}}$$

Chi-Square ($\chi^2$) Goodness-of-Fit Test Statistic:

$$\chi^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{(O_i - E_i)^2}{E_i}$$

Data Interpretation: Analyzing statistical significance ($p$-value $< 0.05$), avoiding spurious correlations, and drawing objective conclusions.

1.8 Necessary Instrumentations

Instrumentation in Computer Science research comprises the full hardware, software, and benchmark stack required for empirical validation:

  • Software Tools: Profilers (Valgrind, TensorBoard, PySpy), statistical environments (R, Python - NumPy/SciPy/Pandas), R packages.
  • Hardware Infrastructure: High-performance GPU nodes (NVIDIA A100/H100), Cloud Compute clusters (AWS EC2, Google Cloud TPU), Oscilloscopes/Logic Analyzers (for IoT/Embedded research).
  • Benchmark Datasets: Standardized validation datasets accepted by the research community (ImageNet, MNIST, CIFAR-100, MS-COCO).

2.3 Plagiarism: Nature, Types & UGC Regulations

Plagiarism is the unacknowledged appropriation of another person's ideas, processes, results, text, or source code without giving proper credit or citation.

Figure 2.2: Comprehensive Taxonomy of Academic Plagiarism
graph TD P[Plagiarism Taxonomy] --> D[Direct / Verbatim] P --> M[Mosaic / Patchwriting] P --> PA[Paraphrase Plagiarism] P --> S[Self-Plagiarism / Text Recycling] P --> C[Cyber-Plagiarism / Code Theft]
Similarity Index vs. Plagiarism: Similarity Index is a raw automated text-match percentage produced by software (e.g. Turnitin), including properly cited quotes and equations. Plagiarism is an ethical breach of intellectual theft.

UGC Academic Integrity Guidelines & Penalties

Level Similarity Threshold UGC Prescribed Penalty
Level 0 Up to 10% Minor similarity — Acceptable threshold; no penalty.
Level 1 Above 10% to 40% Student must submit a revised manuscript within 6 months.
Level 2 Above 40% to 60% Student is debarred from submitting revised script for 1 year.
Level 3 Above 60% Student's registration for the academic program is cancelled.

2.4 Research Ethics

Research ethics governs the moral conduct of scientific enquiry. The FFP Triad represents the primary forms of research misconduct:

  • Fabrication: Making up synthetic data or experimental logs and reporting them as genuine empirical results.
  • Falsification: Manipulating equipment, execution parameters, or figures to distort actual outcomes.
  • Plagiarism: Stealing intellectual ideas, text, or code without proper attribution.

Authorship Ethics (ICMJE Criteria): Co-authorship requires satisfying all 4 criteria: substantial research contribution, manuscript drafting/revision, final approval, and accountability. Unethical practices include Ghost, Gift/Honorary, and Guest authorship.

Technical Writing, Research Proposals & Peer Review

3.1 Effective Technical Writing

Technical writing demands precision, objectivity, clarity, conciseness, and logical flow. Scientific papers follow the universal IMRaD Structural Framework:

Figure 3.1: Standard IMRaD Manuscript Architecture
graph TD Title[Title, Abstract, Keywords] --> I[Introduction: Problem & Motivation] I --> M[Methods: Proposed Architecture & Experiments] M --> R[Results: Benchmark Tables & Charts] R --> D[Discussion: Analysis & Limitations] D --> C[Conclusion & Future Scope]

Structured Abstract Components: Context (1-2 sentences), Problem (1 sentence), Proposed Solution (2-3 sentences), Key Quantitative Results (2 sentences), and Conclusion (1 sentence).

3.2 How to Write Report

Writing a formal research report or master's thesis requires structured chapter organization:

  1. Front Matter: Title Page, Certificate, Abstract, Table of Contents, List of Figures/Tables.
  2. Chapter 1: Introduction: Context, Problem Statement, Objectives, Delimitations, Thesis Outline.
  3. Chapter 2: Literature Survey: Critical appraisal of prior art and research gap identification.
  4. Chapter 3: Proposed Methodology: Algorithmic formulation, architecture diagrams, mathematical proofs.
  5. Chapter 4: Implementation & Experimental Results: Benchmark datasets, performance graphs, baseline comparisons.
  6. Chapter 5: Conclusion & Future Scope: Primary contributions and open directions.
  7. Back Matter: References (IEEE/APA format), Appendices, Published Papers.

3.4 Developing a Research Proposal & Format of Research Proposal

A Research Proposal is a formal document soliciting research grant funding from institutional or governmental bodies (DST-SERB, AICTE, CSIR, ISRO, DRDO, UGC). Standard 7-part format:

Proposal Head Required Technical Contents
1. Executive Summary High-level synthesis of project goals, total budget requirement, duration, and deliverables.
2. Statement of Problem Technical breakdown of the gap, industrial significance, and motivation.
3. Literature Survey & Gap Critical analysis proving no existing commercial product handles the problem.
4. Specific Objectives Bullet-pointed, quantifiable technical targets at major milestones.
5. Work Plan & Methodology Phase-wise tasks supported by a Gantt Chart and PERT network diagram.
6. Budget Breakdown Equipment/Hardware, Manpower (JRF salaries), Travel, Contingency, Overheads.
7. Expected Deliverables Patents, open-source software, journal papers, technology transfer prototypes.

Defending a proposal or thesis before a review committee (Doctoral Advisory Committee / Grant Evaluation Board) focuses on 5 core evaluation criteria: Scientific Novelty, Methodological Rigor, Budget Justification, Risk Mitigation & Feasibility, and Investigator Track Record.

4.1 Nature of Intellectual Property: Patents, Designs, Trade Marks & Copyright

Intellectual Property refers to legal monopoly rights granted by the state for human intellectual creations, balancing private commercial incentive with public domain disclosure.

IP Category Protected Subject Matter Term / Duration Governing Legislation Core Grant Criterion
Patents Functional inventions (Products, Processes). 20 Years ($T_{\text{patent}} = t_{\text{filing}} + 20$) The Patents Act, 1970 Novelty, Non-obviousness, Industrial Utility.
Industrial Designs Aesthetic visual shape or pattern of an article. 10 + 5 Years The Designs Act, 2000 Novel visual appearance, non-functional.
Trade Marks & Secrets Brand names, logos, symbols, slogans / Confidential info. 10 Years (Renewable) / Indefinite The Trade Marks Act, 1999 / Contract Law Distinctiveness; non-confusing / Secrecy.
Copyright Literary, artistic works, software source code. Life + 60 Years The Copyright Act, 1957 Originality of expression.

4.2 Process of Patenting and Development: Technological Research, Innovation, Patenting, Development

The innovation pipeline moves through 4 distinct stages:

  1. Technological Research: Basic scientific enquiry and algorithm design.
  2. Innovation: Applying research to solve a specific practical problem with technical contribution.
  3. Patenting: Novelty searching, filing patent applications, claims drafting, and securing grant.
  4. Development & Commercialization: Technology transfer, licensing, scaling prototype to industrial production.

Statutory Criteria for Patentability (Section 2(1)(j))

  1. Novelty: Absolute world novelty; no prior public disclosure anywhere in the world.
  2. Inventive Step (Non-Obviousness): Technical advance not obvious to a PHOSITA (Person Having Ordinary Skill In The Art).
  3. Industrial Applicability: Capable of industrial manufacture or application.
Non-Patentable Subject Matter (Section 3 & 4): Frivolous inventions (Sec 3a), scientific principles/abstract theories (Sec 3b), computer programs per se or algorithms (Sec 3k), aesthetic creations (Sec 3m), and atomic energy (Sec 4).

4.3 International Scenario: International Cooperation on Intellectual Property

Patents are territorial rights. International cooperation harmonizes IP protection across borders:

  • WIPO (World Intellectual Property Organization): UN specialized agency administering global IP treaties.
  • Paris Convention (1883): Establishes 12-month Priority Right across member states.
  • Berne Convention (1886): International copyright protection without formal registration requirements.
  • TRIPS Agreement (1994): Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights under WTO establishing 20-year minimum patent terms globally.

UNIT V

Patent Rights, Technology Transfer & Emerging IPR

5.1 Patent Rights: Scope of Patent Rights

A patent grants exclusive negative rights to exclude third parties from making, using, selling, or importing the patented product or process. Statutory exceptions include Research Exemption, Bolar Provisions (regulatory data testing), and Compulsory Licensing (Section 84) granted after 3 years if public requirements are unmet or prices unaffordable.

5.2 Licensing and Transfer of Technology

Technology Transfer models enable commercial exploitation of IP:

  • Assignment: Outright permanent transfer/sale of IP title and ownership.
  • Exclusive License: Single licensee gains commercial rights; licensor is excluded from commercial use.
  • Non-Exclusive License: Rights granted to multiple competing entities simultaneously.
  • Cross-Licensing: Mutual exchange of patent portfolio rights between technology firms.

5.3 Patent Information and Databases

Patent landscape searches prevent reinventing the wheel. Major global search engines: InPASS (India), Espacenet (EPO - 140M+ docs), PATENTSCOPE (WIPO), USPTO, and Google Patents. Indexed via IPC (International Patent Classification) and CPC (Cooperative Patent Classification).

5.4 Geographical Indications

A Geographical Indication (GI) identifies goods originating from a specific geographic region with unique qualities or reputation (e.g. Darjeeling Tea, Mysore Silk, Hyderabad Haleem, Pochampally Ikat). GIs are collective property of regional artisans, unlike corporate Trademarks.

5.5 New Developments in IPR: Administration of Patent System

Modernization of patent offices: E-filing portals, digital patents, AI-assisted prior art screening, expedited examination for startups and female inventors.

5.6 New Developments in IPR: IPR of Biological Systems, Computer Software etc.

  • IPR of Biological Systems: Microorganisms are patentable following Diamond v. Chakrabarty (1980). The Budapest Treaty mandates biological sample deposit at an International Depository Authority (MTCC in India).
  • IPR of Computer Software: Software per se is excluded under Section 3(k), but software demonstrating a Technical Effect embedded in hardware is patentable.
  • AI & Inventorship: In test cases involving DABUS, patent offices ruled that an inventor under law must be a natural human person.

5.7 Traditional Knowledge Case Studies

Biopiracy is the unauthorized commercial patenting of indigenous traditional knowledge. India created the Traditional Knowledge Digital Library (TKDL) containing 450,000+ formulations in 5 international languages to prevent bad patents globally.

Landmark Case Contested Patent Indian Defense Evidence Outcome
Turmeric Case (1997) US Patent on wound healing. Ancient Sanskrit texts proving prior art in India. Patent Revoked by USPTO.
Neem Case (2000) EPO Patent on anti-fungal extract. Evidence of traditional agricultural usage. Patent Revoked by EPO.
Basmati Rice (2001) US Patent on Basmati lines. Genetic strain data & geographic origin evidence. RiceTec forced to withdraw major claims.

5.8 IPR and IITs

Top Indian universities (IITs, IISc) enforce IP policies: IP developed using institutional infrastructure is owned by the Institute; commercial royalties are split (typically 70% to Inventors / 30% to Institution); faculty/student spin-offs are supported via incubation parks (FITT, SINE, IITM RP).

Q2. b) Discuss the errors in selecting a research problem. [5M, CO1, BL3] — Booklet Target: ~2 Pages
Detailed Model Answer for Booklet:

1. Introduction: Improper selection of a research problem leads to wasted effort, resource exhaustion, and potential project abandonment. Recognizing common errors ensures a feasible and impactful research trajectory.

2. Major Errors in Problem Selection:

  • Over-Broad & Unbounded Scope ("Solving World Hunger" Fallacy): Formulating a topic so vast (e.g. "Improving Cybersecurity using Artificial Intelligence") that it cannot be investigated within time and resource bounds. A good problem must be tightly delimited.
  • Trivial & Over-Researched Problems: Selecting topics that have already been extensively solved or re-implementing established baselines without adding novel architectural or algorithmic value.
  • Ignoring Compute, Hardware & Resource Constraints: Formulating problems requiring unavailable infrastructure (e.g. attempting to train a 70-billion parameter LLM from scratch without dedicated GPU clusters like NVIDIA A100/H100) or proprietary datasets.
  • Unscientific / Subjective Personal Bias: Designing a problem to confirm a pre-conceived personal opinion rather than uncovering objective, empirical, and verifiable truth.
  • Ignoring Data Availability & Privacy Regulations: Formulating problems reliant on sensitive human data without verifying data privacy compliance (GDPR, IRB clearance).

OR — Q3. Analyze the approaches of investigation of solutions for a research problem with respect to data collection, analysis and interpretation. [10M, CO1, BL4] — Booklet Target: ~3-4 Pages
Detailed Model Answer for Booklet:

1. Introduction: Investigating solutions for a research problem requires a structured, empirical framework spanning logical reasoning pathways, data collection, statistical analysis, and objective interpretation.

2. Investigation Logic Pathways:

  • Deductive Reasoning (Top-Down): Moves from General Theory $\rightarrow$ Hypothesis Formulation ($H_0: \mu_{\text{proposed}} = \mu_{\text{baseline}}$ vs $H_1: \mu_{\text{proposed}} > \mu_{\text{baseline}}$) $\rightarrow$ Controlled Experimentation $\rightarrow$ Confirmation/Rejection. Used to validate existing theories under new conditions.
  • Inductive Reasoning (Bottom-Up): Moves from Specific Observations $\rightarrow$ Pattern Recognition $\rightarrow$ Tentative Hypothesis $\rightarrow$ General Theory Building. Used when exploring under-researched domains.

3. Data Collection Framework:

  • Primary Data Collection: Gathered directly via controlled experiments, hardware sensor logs, user telemetry, or network packet traces. Provides high authenticity.
  • Secondary Data Collection: Extracted from standardized benchmark repositories (Kaggle, UCI ML Repository, IEEE DataPort).
  • Sample Size Determination (Cochran's Formula): $$n_0 = \frac{Z^2 \cdot p \cdot (1 - p)}{e^2}$$ For finite populations, Yamane's correction is applied: $n = \frac{N}{1 + N(e)^2}$.

4. Statistical Data Analysis:

  • Descriptive Statistics: Mean, median, standard deviation, and variance summarizing benchmark execution metrics.
  • Inferential Hypothesis Testing (Student's t-test): $$t = \frac{\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2}{\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}}$$
  • Chi-Square ($\chi^2$) Test of Independence: $$\chi^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{(O_i - E_i)^2}{E_i}$$

5. Data Interpretation: Evaluating $p$-value statistical significance ($p < 0.05$), isolating confounding variables, avoiding correlation-causation fallacies, and stating research delimitations.

OR — Q5. Evaluate the issue of plagiarism in research and recommend suitable measures to prevent it. [10M, CO2, BL5] — Booklet Target: ~3-4 Pages
Detailed Model Answer for Booklet:

1. Definition: Plagiarism is the unacknowledged appropriation of another person's ideas, text, empirical results, or code without proper attribution.

2. Comprehensive Plagiarism Taxonomy:

  • Direct / Verbatim Plagiarism: Word-for-word copy-pasting without quotation marks or attribution.
  • Mosaic / Patchwriting Plagiarism: Intertwining copied phrases with personal synonyms while keeping original structure.
  • Paraphrase Plagiarism: Rewording another author's core ideas without citing the source.
  • Self-Plagiarism (Text Recycling): Re-publishing one's own previously published text/data without disclosure.
  • Cyber-Plagiarism / Code Theft: Copying open-source software scripts without license compliance.

3. Similarity Index vs. Plagiarism: Similarity Index is an automated text-matching percentage generated by software (Turnitin, iThenticate). It includes properly cited quotes and standard formulas. Plagiarism is an ethical breach of intellectual theft.
